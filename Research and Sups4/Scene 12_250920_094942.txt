Section XII — Weaving the ENTROPY/INFO Rail into the Shell & Using the 4-bit Qubit-Cap
(Goal: every move carries an explicit information budget; local fractures get fixed with a tiny, reversible 4-bit “cap” that preserves global parity while stabilizing the hot zone. Same CQE cadence, now with thermodynamics-of-information riding shotgun.)
0) Quick Q&A warm-up
Q: Why add an info rail if the physics rails already pass?
A: Because passing can still be wasteful. The info rail tells you where you’re throwing bits (and heat). It makes Δ-lifts targeted (max information gain per unit cost) and prevents “green-by-threshold” wins that silently erase evidence.
Q: Is this about quantum? I’m not building a quantum computer.
A: No quantum hardware needed. We use information quantities (Shannon , mutual information , KL divergence , von Neumann where relevant) plus Landauer: erasing one bit costs heat. That’s a design budget, not a lab constraint.
Q: Won’t this slow me down?
A: It speeds you up by aiming the viewer. The info rail ranks hot zones by “most fixable, most informative.” You spend Δ-lifts where the expected mutual information bump is highest.

2) The 4-bit qubit-cap (the smallest reversible repair that matters)
What it is: a 16-state reversible micro-operator you can drop onto a single hot tile (and its mirror mate) to stabilize without global rework. Think of it as a local isometry in a 2×2 parity cell:
• State space: 4 bits → reversible maps (we constrain to parity-respecting variants).
• Actions you encode with it (choose one): swap, invert, phase flip, orthogonalize, renormalize, tiny rotation, binary mask, basis re-index.
• Why “qubit-cap”? It behaves like a tiny, reversible gate cap: you patch the tile while keeping mirror/parity intact—no entropy dump unless you later choose to erase.
When to use:
• 8×8 local viewer flags a persistent red/amber.
• 4×4×4×4 surround is green/amber (local, not global).
• 2×2×2×2 parity surround passes (so a local reversible nudge is legal).
What to log: the cap ID (one of 16), pre/post bit ledger (Δ info, Δ residual), and mirror parity check marks. If you later erase a cap (commit to an irreversible simplification), you log a Landauer debit.

4) The three-step cap protocol (so you don’t overuse it)
• Screening: cap only if local 8×8 is red/amber, 4×4×4×4 is green/amber, and 2×2×2×2 parity is green.
• Choice: pick one of 16 parity-respecting cap maps (documented menu: swap, invert, rotate , orthogonalize, renorm, mask, re-index, phase flip, etc.).
• Audit: recompute mirror & info receipts; if MI ↓ or KL ↑ in neighbors, revert (caps must be net-beneficial).
Caps are reversible patches. Δ-lifts are model rewrites. Strict ratchets are maturity locks. Don’t mix their roles.

7) Pitfalls (and their antidotes)
• Green-by-smoothing: Passing a view by destroying MI. Antidote: require MI non-decrease (or a logged Landauer debit) for any pass.
• Cap addiction: Over-patching instead of fixing. Antidote: at most one cap per tile; two caps in the same 8×8 → write a Δ-lift.
• Global fracture disguised as local: Parity surround red means stop capping; re-pose the view or split the band.
