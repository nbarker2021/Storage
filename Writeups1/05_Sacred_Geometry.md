## 2. Mathematical Foundations: Digital Roots and Geometric Music Theory

### 2.1 Digital Roots

The **digital root** of a number is the single-digit value obtained by an iterative process of summing digits, on a given base. For example, the digital root of 432 is 4+3+2=9. The digital root of 528 is 5+2+8=15, and 1+5=6. In base 10, the digital root of a number is equivalent to its remainder when divided by 9 (with the exception of a digital root of 9, which corresponds to a remainder of 0) [2, 3].

Digital roots have several interesting properties. For example, the digital root of the product of two numbers is the digital root of the product of their digital roots. This property, known as "casting out nines," has been used for centuries as a quick way to check arithmetic.

### 2.2 Geometric Music Theory

**Geometric music theory** is a branch of music theory that uses geometric concepts to model musical phenomena. It represents notes, chords, and scales as points in a geometric space, and musical transformations (such as transposition and inversion) as symmetries of that space [4, 5].

This approach allows for a deeper understanding of the structure of music and has led to new insights into the nature of harmony and voice leading. The CQE system extends this concept, representing not just musical notes but all forms of data as points in the geometric space of the E8 lattice.

## 5. A Deeper Look at Digital Roots and Modulo Arithmetic

The properties of digital roots are a direct consequence of modulo arithmetic. The digital root of a number *n* is simply *n* mod 9 (with the result 0 being mapped to 9). This is because any power of 10 is congruent to 1 mod 9. For example, 100 = 11*9 + 1, so 100 ≡ 1 (mod 9).

This means that a number like 432 can be written as:

4*100 + 3*10 + 2*1 ≡ 4*1 + 3*1 + 2*1 ≡ 9 ≡ 0 (mod 9)

This connection to modulo arithmetic is what gives digital roots their power. It allows us to analyze the properties of large numbers by looking at their much simpler digital roots.
